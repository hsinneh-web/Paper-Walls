Paper Walls: The Architecture of Control is a literary memoir that examines how identity, belonging, and truth are shaped inside systems that prioritize narrative over lived experience. Raised in a carefully curated Los Angeles household where appearance governed acceptance, the author traces how concern becomes narrative, narrative becomes record, and record becomes enforcement. What begins as a family story expands into a precise study of authority, perception, and the quiet mechanisms that determine who gets to define reality. Alongside the memoir is the Paper Walls Record Archive—a parallel body of documentation drawn from court filings, public records, and historical fragments connected to the Om group. Where the memoir holds what was lived, the archive presents what could be processed: identities assigned, facts arranged, histories stabilized across systems. It does not reconcile contradictions or resolve competing accounts. It preserves them. Together, they form a complete structure: experience and documentation, memory and record, truth and access. The two do not collapse into a single version. They exist in tension, revealing the gap between what is known and what can be confirmed. This is not a story about what happened. It is a record of how reality is constructed—and what it takes to step outside of it. Paper Walls: The Architecture of Control
